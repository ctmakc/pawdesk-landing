PawDesk vs Spreadsheets

Why spreadsheets fail groomers

Almost every groomer we talked to started with a spreadsheet. It seems logical — you already have Google Sheets or Excel, it's free, and you can customize it however you want. For the first ten clients, it works. Then the wheels fall off.

The problem isn't that spreadsheets are bad tools. The problem is that they're not appointment books, not invoicing systems, and not client databases — and trying to make them all three at once means you end up with something fragile that only you understand, which breaks the moment you're busy.

Here's what groomers tell us goes wrong with spreadsheets:

  • Appointment conflicts because the spreadsheet doesn't enforce double-booking rules
  • No automatic reminders — you're texting 12 clients every Sunday night
  • Invoice tracking is a separate spreadsheet, which drifts out of sync
  • Pet notes are buried in cells that are impossible to find on your phone mid-groom
  • No photos, no voice notes, no per-visit history
  • Everything is lost if you accidentally delete a row

PawDesk replaces the spreadsheet without the learning curve. Everything that was a manual cell entry is now automatic. Appointments book with conflict detection, invoices generate in one tap, and pet notes are always one search away. And it works on your phone — not just on a laptop.

The verdict: Spreadsheets are free and flexible but become a liability as your business grows. PawDesk is also free during beta and built specifically for how groomers work. There's no reason to keep the spreadsheet. See what PawDesk includes.

PawDesk vs Square Appointments

Square Appointments is built for salons, not groomers

Square Appointments is a polished product. If you're a hair salon or a nail bar, it's probably the right choice. But if you're a dog groomer, you're going to spend a lot of time working around what it doesn't do.

Square doesn't understand the concept of a pet. There's no place to log a pet's breed, coat type, allergies, or visit history. Every time a client walks in, you're starting from scratch — hoping you remember which shampoo Bella needs or that Max bites when you touch his back left paw.

Key differences between PawDesk and Square Appointments for groomers:

  • PawDesk has pet profiles with photos, coat type, allergies, and full visit history. Square does not.
  • PawDesk includes voice notes you can dictate hands-free after each groom. Square does not.
  • PawDesk is free during beta. Square Appointments starts at $29/month plus payment processing fees.
  • PawDesk is mobile-first and works offline. Square requires a reliable connection for most functions.
  • Square is stronger for retail point-of-sale and brick-and-mortar payments. If you have a full retail counter, Square may still make sense for the register. PawDesk is better for everything grooming-specific.

The verdict: Square is a great all-purpose small business tool that wasn't designed for groomers. PawDesk was. If grooming is your primary business — not a side add-on to a retail operation — PawDesk gives you features Square can't match at a price that's hard to argue with.

PawDesk vs Acuity Scheduling

Acuity handles bookings but not your business

Acuity Scheduling is one of the most popular online booking tools for service businesses. It's slick, well-supported, and integrates with everything. It's also firmly in the "generic booking tool" category — which means it's designed to work for personal trainers, photographers, therapists, and wedding planners equally. That breadth is its strength and its weakness.

For groomers, Acuity solves the scheduling and reminder problem well. Clients can book online, pick a time slot, and get automatic confirmations. Where it falls short is everything that happens after the booking — the groom itself, the notes, the invoice, and the client relationship.

  • Acuity has no pet profiles. There's no structured way to store an animal's breed, coat condition, or care history.
  • Invoicing in Acuity requires a separate tool (FreshBooks, Wave, or similar) or manual entry. PawDesk invoices from the same app as your calendar.
  • Acuity has no voice notes feature. Post-groom notes have to be typed manually in a notes field — if it exists at all.
  • Acuity's mobile app is functional but not mobile-first. PawDesk is designed from the ground up for one-handed phone use.
  • Acuity starts at $20/month. PawDesk is free during beta and designed specifically for groomers at a comparable price when paid plans launch.

The verdict: If online client booking (where clients book themselves) is your primary need, Acuity is strong. If you prefer to manage bookings yourself — which most independent groomers do — PawDesk's scheduling is just as capable, and it adds everything Acuity lacks: pet profiles, visit history, voice notes, and integrated invoicing.
