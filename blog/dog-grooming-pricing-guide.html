Dog grooming pricing guide 2026: what to charge and why

May 16, 2026 · 12 min read · By the PawDesk team

One of the hardest things about running an independent grooming business is knowing what to charge. You don't want to price yourself out of the market. You don't want to work for less than your time is worth. And unlike an employee, no one hands you a pay stub and tells you what your labor is worth — you have to figure it out yourself.

This guide covers 2026 market rates across North America, the factors that affect where you should sit in that range, how to raise prices correctly, and why undercharging is a bigger long-term risk than overcharging.

Current market rates by breed and service (2026)

The ranges below reflect what independent groomers are charging across major Canadian and US cities in 2026. Mobile groomers typically add $15-25 to these rates for travel and the convenience premium. Rates in rural areas tend to be 10-20% lower.

What should put you at the top of the range

Within each breed and service category, there's a wide spread. Groomers at the top of the range share certain characteristics:

  • Location and market. Toronto, Vancouver, Calgary, and major US metros support higher rates than smaller cities. Know your local market.
  • Experience and certifications. Groomers with 5+ years of experience and professional certifications (National Dog Groomers Association of America, Canadian Professional Pet Stylists) can charge a premium.
  • Specialization. Asian-style grooming, hand-scissor work, senior dog care, and anxiety-free grooming for reactive dogs all command premium rates.
  • Professional experience. Clean, professional booking, invoicing, and communication make clients willing to pay more. If you use dedicated grooming management software and send professional invoices, you feel like a premium service.
  • Waitlist demand. If you're turning away clients, raise your prices until the waitlist shrinks to a manageable level. This is a direct signal from the market that you're undercharging.

How to raise your prices without losing clients

Price increases are one of the most anxiety-inducing things in the grooming business. In practice, done correctly, they almost never cause the client exodus groomers fear.

The correct process for a price increase:

  • Give at least 30 days' notice. Send a brief, professional message explaining that your rates are going up on [date]. A sentence explaining why (cost of supplies, inflation, product quality) is enough. Don't over-explain or apologize.
  • Increase by 10-15% at a time, not 30% at once. Gradual increases over time are easier to absorb.
  • Honor the old rate for your longest-tenured clients for one more visit if you want to soften the transition. But don't feel obligated — it's your business.
  • Be matter-of-fact, not apologetic. Clients take their cue from you. If you present the increase confidently and professionally, they will accept it as normal. If you hedge and apologize, they'll start negotiating.

Why undercharging is riskier than overcharging

Most groomers default to the lower end of the rate range because they're afraid of losing clients. But undercharging creates a set of problems that are harder to escape than the occasional client who leaves over price.

When you undercharge, you have to do more grooms to earn the same income. More grooms means more physical strain. Grooming is physically demanding — repetitive bending, lifting, standing for hours. The groomers who burn out in their 30s and 40s are almost always the ones who were working at high volume and low margin. The ones still thriving in their 60s are typically the ones who raised their prices early, did fewer grooms, and did each one with more care.

Undercharging also attracts price-sensitive clients — the ones who shop around, negotiate, complain most about small things, and leave when they find someone $5 cheaper. High-rate clients tend to be more loyal, more appreciative, and more likely to refer friends. They're buying trust and expertise, not a commodity.

The math is simple: two grooms at $90 is the same revenue as three grooms at $60. The $90 scenario leaves you with an extra hour, less physical strain, and clients who are more likely to rebook and refer. See the full guide to growing your grooming business.
